Pre-class activity · search algorithms

How many tries do you need to find a number?

Before seeing the algorithms in class, you're going to discover them yourself. Complete both phases, answer the questions, and compare your results. The activity takes about 50 minutes.

1
Phase 1 · shuffled cards. Find a number among 16 cards in random order — no hints. Then again with 70 cards. 3 rounds each.
2
Phase 2 · sorted cards. Cards go from lowest to highest. If you miss, you'll know whether the number is to the right or left. 16 cards, then 70 cards. 3 rounds each.
3
Reflection & comparison. Guided questions to help you discover why there's such a big difference between the two strategies.

Phase 2 · sorted · 16 cards

Now the cards are sorted

Two changes compared to Phase 1:

✦
Cards are sorted from lowest to highest. You can see the position of each card (1 = lowest value, 16 = highest). You don't know the exact value, but you know its place in the order.
✦
If you miss, you get a hint. Eliminated cards change colour. Red ↑ = yours is higher. Green ↓ = yours is lower.

Key question before you begin: if you know the number is in the right half of the deck, which card is the smartest to flip to eliminate the most options in one go?

Phase 2 · Round 2 · 70 cards

Binary search with 70 sorted cards

Same strategy as before — but now with 70 sorted cards. Remember: always pick the middle of the remaining range!

With 16 sorted cards the strategy needed at most 4 steps. How many do you think 70 cards will need? Think before you start!

✦
70 cards, sorted lowest to highest. Position numbers are visible. Hints tell you higher ↑ or lower ↓ on every miss.
✦
Apply the halving strategy. Always pick the card at the midpoint of whatever range is still active.

Final result

What you just discovered has a name

What you did in Phase 1
Linear search
Checks elements one by one until the target is found. Requires no prior order.
Worst case: N steps
16 cards → up to 16 · 70 cards → up to 70
What you did in Phase 2
Binary search
Each step eliminates half the remaining options. Requires sorted data.
Worst case: log₂(N) steps
16 cards → up to 4 · 70 cards → up to 7

With 16 cards, binary search needs at most 4 steps (2⁴ = 16). With 70 cards, just 7 steps (2⁷ = 128 > 70). With 1,000,000 elements only 20 steps (2²⁰ ≈ 1,000,000). How many steps would linear search need in the worst case with a million elements?
